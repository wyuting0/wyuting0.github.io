Becoming an iCouncillor was a journey that was both challenging and rewarding. It wasn't smooth sailing all the way, and there certainly were many challenges along the way to get to where I am now. Even with all the late nights and overthinking, every bit of effort and hard work paid off.

## March Camp

My journey began during the March holidays with the first round of selection called March Camp, which took place over two days on March 11 and 12. Many of my classmates and friends signed up for the iCouncil selection camp, so I decided to join in as well. 

March Camp was pretty easygoing and casual compared to what was to come. We played many different team-building games and icebreakers, which included debating and charades. It was interesting and enjoyable to engage with my peers, getting to know them better, making new friends. There wasn't that much pressure or hard work; I must say at that time that I really didn't care much either about getting in or not. It was a fun and laid-back experience, which made it a great start to my journey.

At the very end of the second day, we faced a challenging task. All iCiTs were tasked to create a play about a specific place in our school. There were easy ones like the clock tower or canteen, but we got the black box, a rather obscure room used for just two purposes: ACC (Appreciation of Chinese Culture) classes and Chinese Drama CCA sessions.

We wasted a ton time at the beginning by walking down to the black box which was all the way at the SALT Centre, where we found it was locked. That really made thinking about ideas problematic, and it wasn't until almost halfway through that we really began to get a clear plan. We ran back and did as much as we could with the remaining time. By the end, we had only gotten down a rough outline of our script, and most of the play had to be improvised.

Fortunately, our group was scheduled to be one of the last to perform, which allowed extra time for the completion of our ideas. We were able to come up with a performance that showed the ACC classes in school and Chinese Drama CCA sessions after school. I was the narrator, providing context and adding to the story, while other group members portrayed students, teachers, and one as the black box itself. Our performance turned out pretty good, despite being in a rush, and became the most memorable part of March Camp.

At the end of the camp, there was an award ceremony for all the groups. While my classmates in other groups received meaningful awards like "Best Teamwork Award" and "自强不息 Award," my group was the last to be called and ended up with the disappointing and honestly quite amusing "Rising Star Award."

## Summer Retreat

The real challenge began long before the Summer Retreat itself. About a month before the Summer Retreat, all iCiTs who had passed the first round of selection were given a major task: to create a fictional event called "Leadership UNLEASHED!". "Leadership UNLEASHED!" is a leadership camp we had to fully plan out that would put ourr administrative skills and time management to the test. We were tasked to prepare a proposal document for this 3-day event in which 100 student leaders from all over the world would be invited for presentation, discussion, and the speech of successful leaders. Not only that, we had to create a poster promoting the event and facilitator slides for it as well.

Seriously, no words could describe how much effort I put into this project. We had to think carefully about every detail, from the schedule to logistics, so that everything is educative and entertaining. That tested how well we could plan, organize, and work as a team. I knew that the quality of the proposal would play a significant role in whether or not I would get selected, so I put in my best effort. I would grind every single day, constantly refining and updating the proposal.

However, not everyone in my team was equally invested. Of the five people in our group, only three of us really contributed. We really tried to convince the others to get started, but it seemed like they didn't care. It was just so many tiring-some nights, up all night into the next day, trying to get everything done. Regardless, we managed to cram well and completed the project. Funnily, we uploaded it less than an hour before it was due.

The next day, we returned to school for the Summer Retreat selection camp. It was, once again, a two-day long selection camp. But this time, the pressure was real. I was constantly scared of the possibility that one mistake would completely destroy my chances of getting in, and all the time and effort I had put into the planning of "Leadership UNLEASHED!" would be wasted. You could say I was paranoid. Feelings aside, we played many icebreakers and team-building games which were pretty fun. Working in our group to complete simple tasks felt like a breath of fresh air from the challenge of planning a massive project online. The only obstacle we really faced was the planning of the business proposal, which we were assigned to do on the second day of the camp. Our group was struggling to think of ideas but we eventually settled on pets to combat loneliness faced by the elderly. Unfortunately, we made a mistake: our proposal was designed as a non-profit, which meant it would incur costs rather than generate profit. We were quite harshly criticized by the judges, which really brought down my morale.

At the end of the Summer Retreat, there was another award ceremony for all the groups. Once again, my group called last and received the "Most Enthusiastic Group" award. It felt a lot like the "Rising Star Award" I received during March Camp—just a filler or consolation prize for the worst-performing group.

## Two Months of Anticipation

Now that the selection camps were fianlly over, all I could do was wait. The two-month period until the release of the results felt like an eternity. I spent much of that time reflecting on my performance as I tried to manage my expectations. On one hand, I contributed a lot to the "Leadership UNLEASHED!" project, but on the other hand, our group’s performance was rather lacklustre, especially the business proposal.

Those two months were filled with contemplation and self-reflection. During that long wait, I would frequently find myself replaying every moment from both selection camps, thinking about what went well and what did not. I tried to convince myself that all the dedication and hard work I had put in would be recognised, but uncertainty was always lurking in the back of my mind.

## The Big Day

On August 22, after I had just finished my final French lesson of the year, I checked my phone to see an unexpected notification. It was a message sent in the iCouncillors-in-Training (iCiTs) group chat. It read, "@all - iCiTs, please meet the teacher mentors outside LT3 at 9.45 am tomorrow." Even though the message was quite vague, my heart skipped a beat. It was evident the results were going to be released the next day.

The next morning, I arrived outside LT3 early. I saw a few of my classmates as well as some group members, equally tense and worried. At exactly 9:45 am, the teacher mentors arrived, and the iCiTs were called together. As I walked into the lecture theatre, I prepared to hold my breath. The mentors spoke briefly about the council, emphasising that it wouldn't be the only leadership opportunity for for those who did not get selected. Then, they flashed the list of selected iCouncillors on the screen. My heart raced as I scanned the list, and there it was—my name.

## Conclusion

From March Camp to Summer Retreat, every part of the selection process was a challenge that tested me. Sometimes I felt unsure and frustrated, but I learned to accept the process and stay focused. Even when things were hard, I found the strength to keep trying. This experience has helped me believe in myself and try my best in everything I do.
