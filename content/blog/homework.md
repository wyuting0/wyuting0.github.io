Have you ever thought of the point of homework? Are those assignments that keep on piling up, the late nights spent struggling to get through them, and the seemingly never-ending cycle of work contributing anything to education or are they just a burden to students’ life? I sure have. Whether it is tuition or school work, I can think of a million reasons why there shouldn’t be any homework in the first place. Every hour comes with an irritating question that makes me tremble with fear every single time it pops into my mind; did I complete all my homework? The mere thought of lingers like a ghoul haunting each moment especially during weekends.

## Evolution of Homework

Homework has been a long-standing component in education dating back to 19th century. Roberto Nevilis has often received recognition as its pioneer. An Italian instructor who assigned the first official home task in 1905 is regarded as its inventor by most scholars. However, during early 20th century, industrialisation compelled United States’ educators to develop structured educational system.

As the educational landscape evolved, so did the perception of homework. Initially considered a tool for reinforcing classroom learning, it gradually transformed into a metric for academic success. This trend soon evolved into a global standard, ruining the childhoods of millions of children like mine. Homework became synonymous with discipline, responsibility, and academic rigour, but the question remains: is it truly serving its intended purpose?

## The Burden

Despite being assigned to help reinforce classroom learning, homework often ends up becoming an overwhelming burden on students. There are numerous problems that arise from this academic excellence pressure such as stress and anxiety among others which leads to poor health condition. Students have to deal with different subjects at once, participate in extracurricular activities and perform personal tasks—all these leave no time for rest and entertainment purposes. This is very important since there is need to know whether the weight carried by homework outweighs any benefits derived from it when looking at matters from that angle.

Research on homework has produced contradictory results over time. In certain studies some indicate that small doses of homework can increase comprehension and performance whereas huge amounts may harm. In addition, the quality of homework assignments as well as how they match up with instructional goals will be vital in determining whether or not they are impactful.

## The Solution

Banning homework could serve as a significant step toward increasing happiness and alleviating the alarming rates of teenage suicides and depression globally. The intense academic pressure and relentless workload imposed by homework contribute to heightened stress levels among students. In Singapore, a country known for its rigorous education system, students face immense competition and expectations, often leading to mental health issues. By eliminating homework, students would gain valuable time for rest, relaxation, and extracurricular activities, fostering a healthier work-life balance. This change could potentially reduce the overwhelming pressure on teenagers, offering them the opportunity to explore their interests, engage in social activities, and develop essential life skills. Ultimately, prioritising mental well-being over excessive academic demands may contribute to a happier and more resilient younger generation, not only in Singapore but across the globe.

## Conclusion

If we take time to imagine a world without the burden of traditional homework, we can then consider a well-rounded and more enjoyable educational journey. Emphasising mental health as number one priority, frees us from rushing through assignments, forcing us to complete them in a deadline. In the grand scheme of education, homework emerges as a downright atrocity, leaving us exasperated with its burdensome weight.
