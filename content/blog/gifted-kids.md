As someone who participated in the Gifted Education Program (GEP) from Primary 4 to Primary 6, let me tell you that it wasn't as magical as everyone would have you believe. Although it may sound cool to be called "gifted" at such a young age, in my experience, it created some pretty unrealistic expectations that had a greater influence on my life than I could have ever imagined.

## The Early Label

It seemed like a golden ticket to success to be accepted into the GEP. Who wouldn't want to be regarded as "gifted," after all? However, in retrospect, I can't help but believe that labeling children at such a young age might not be the best course of action. Getting into a programme that has an acceptance rate of just 0.25% gives us the impression that we have this superpower that's supposed to make everything easy, but reality hits hard when you realize it's not all smooth sailing. When I was in Primary 3, I got into the Gifted Education Program (GEP) after passing rounds 1 and 2. I was over the moon, thinking I was some kind of genius destined for an easy life. Now, looking back, I realize just how foolish that notion was. The harsh reality is that achieving success at an early age is often devoid of true meaning.

## Unrealistic Expectations

It's genuinely mind-boggling how they label you as "gifted," and suddenly, it feels like everyone expects you to be a genius at everything. The pressure to excel in academics just keeps piling up, and it messes with your head big time. When things don't go perfectly, you start questioning your worth, wondering if you're living up to this unrealistic image they've created. It's like you're supposed to have all the answers, but in reality, you're just a kid trying to figure things out. The constant pressure can be overwhelming, and it's tough when you feel like you have to meet these sky-high expectations. This held especially true during my Primary 5 year.

## Stunted Growth

Being in the GEP meant I was surrounded by smart kids, but it also meant that we were all stuck in this bubble of high expectations. It's like we were growing taller in one area but getting stunted in others. Socially, emotionally, and even physically, I felt a bit out of sync with my peers. It's tough when people assume you have it all figured out just because you're "gifted."

## The Real Struggle

Here's the thing - being in the GEP didn't guarantee a smooth ride through life. In fact, it made some things even harder. The fear of not living up to the "gifted" label, the stress of always having to be the best – it took a toll on my mental health. I found myself dealing with anxiety and a constant fear of failure that I never thought would hit me so early on.

## Setting Realistic Expectations

Looking back, I wish they had focused more on nurturing our interests and helping us develop as well-rounded individuals. Instead of pushing us to fit into this "gifted" mold, maybe they could have encouraged us to explore our passions and grow at our own pace. Setting realistic expectations would have saved us from the unnecessary pressure and stress.

## Conclusion

Being in the GEP had its perks, but it also had its downsides. If I could give advice to my younger self, I'd say, don't let the "gifted" label define you. It's okay not to have everything figured out, and it's okay to ask for help. As I recently completed the PSLE this year, I've come to realize that the journey matters just as much as the destination. After all, I wouldn't want to end up being a burnt out perfectionist with depression, anxiety, unfufilled expections and no real strengths and interests outside of academics.
